# Behavioral Interview Guide
### The round most engineers underprepare for — and often fail

At most companies, behavioral rounds are **equally weighted** to coding. One bad behavioral can cancel a great coding performance. This guide gives you the framework, question bank, and example stories to handle it.

---

## The STAR Framework

Every behavioral answer follows this structure:

| Component | What It Is | Time |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| **S**ituation | The context. Project, team, constraint. | 10-15% |
| **T**ask | What YOU specifically were responsible for | 10-15% |
| **A**ction | What YOU did, step by step. Use "I", not "we" | 60-70% |
| **R**esult | Quantified outcome. What changed. What you learned | 15-20% |

**Common mistakes:**
- Too much Situation, not enough Action
- Saying "we" instead of "I" — they're evaluating *you*
- No quantified result ("it went well" → useless)
- Vague actions ("I worked on the problem") → be specific about your decisions

## Amazon's 16 Leadership Principles

Amazon explicitly grades every answer against LPs. Other companies use similar frameworks without naming them. Know these cold.

### The 16 LPs — What They're Actually Asking

| LP | What They Want to See |
|----|----------------------|
| **Customer Obsession** | You start from the customer problem, not the tech solution |
| **Ownership** | You don't say "not my job." You follow through beyond your scope |
| **Invent and Simplify** | You've created something new OR dramatically simplified something complex |
| **Are Right, A Lot** | You have good judgment; you seek data; you update when wrong |
| **Learn and Be Curious** | Self-directed learning; you went beyond what was required |
| **Hire and Develop the Best** | You raised the bar; you mentored; you pushed back on low-bar hires |
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | You pushed quality even when others wanted to cut corners |
| **Think Big** | You've proposed something ambitious; you connected local work to larger vision |
| **Bias for Action** | You moved fast under uncertainty; you didn't wait for perfect info |
| **Frugality** | You delivered with less; you challenged waste |
| **Earn Trust** | You were candid; you gave hard feedback; you admitted mistakes |
| **Dive Deep** | You got into the details; you caught something others missed |
| **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | You pushed back respectfully, then committed once a decision was made |
| **Deliver Results** | Despite obstacles, you hit the goal |
| **Strive to be Earth's Best Employer** | (Mostly for managers — skip unless senior) |
| **Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility** | You thought about impact beyond just the task |

### Minimum Story Bank
Prepare **2 strong stories per LP**. That's a lot — prioritize:
1. Customer Obsession ×2
2. Ownership ×2
3. Deliver Results ×2
4. Disagree and Commit ×2
5. Dive Deep ×2
6. Invent and Simplify ×1
7. Bias for Action ×1
8. Earn Trust ×1

## Universal Question Bank

### Conflict & Disagreement
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a team member / manager / stakeholder."
- "Describe a situation where you had to push back on a decision."
- "Tell me about a time you had to convince someone who was resistant to your idea."
- "Give me an example of a time when you had a conflict with a peer."

**What they're looking for:** That you disagree respectfully and with data, listen, and commit once a decision is made. They do NOT want "I convinced them and they agreed" every time — show that sometimes you were overruled and handled it well.

---

### Failure & Learning
- "Tell me about a time you failed."
- "Describe your biggest professional mistake."
- "Tell me about a project that didn't go as planned."
- "What's something you would do differently?"

**What they're looking for:** Self-awareness, accountability (not blaming others), and concrete changes you made afterward. The lesson must be genuinely learned — not "I learned to communicate better" (too vague).

**Template answer structure:**
> "The mistake was X. I was responsible because [specific action I took]. The impact was [quantified]. I owned it by [what I did to fix it]. Since then, I've changed [specific behavior/process], which led to [concrete outcome]."

---

### Leadership & Influence
- "Tell me about a time you led a project without formal authority."
- "Describe a situation where you had to influence a team outside your control."
- "Tell me about a time you stepped up in a leadership gap."
- "Give an example of when you helped someone on your team grow."

---

### Prioritization & Tradeoffs
- "Tell me about a time you had to prioritize competing demands."
- "Describe a situation where you had to make a decision with incomplete information."
- "How have you handled being asked to do too much at once?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to cut scope to meet a deadline."

---

### Technical Depth
- "Walk me through a technically complex problem you solved."
- "Tell me about a system you designed. What tradeoffs did you make?"
- "Describe a time you improved the performance or reliability of a system."
- "What's the most technically challenging thing you've built?"

---

### Initiative & Ownership
- "Tell me about something you did that wasn't in your job description."
- "Describe a time you identified and solved a problem before being asked."
- "Give an example of when you took ownership of a failing project."
- "Tell me about a process improvement you initiated."

---

### Ambiguity & Fast-Moving Situations
- "Tell me about a time you had to act without having all the information you needed."
- "Describe a situation where the requirements kept changing."
- "How have you handled joining a new team or project that was already in chaos?"

---

### Working with Others
- "Tell me about a difficult teammate. How did you handle it?"
- "Describe a time you had to work with someone very different from you."
- "Tell me about your most successful collaboration."
- "How do you handle receiving critical feedback?"

## Meta-Specific Questions
Meta's behavioral framework is "Situation-Behavior-Impact" (SBI) rather than STAR.
They focus heavily on:
- Speed and efficiency of decision-making
- Data-driven thinking
- Cross-functional collaboration

Key Meta questions:
- "Tell me about a time you moved fast and broke something — and what happened."
- "Describe a decision you made that you're proud of. How did you measure success?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to balance speed vs quality."
- "Give an example of when you used data to change someone's mind."

---

## Google-Specific Questions
Google uses a **structured hiring committee** — multiple interviewers score independently.
They assess: General Cognitive Ability, Leadership, Googleyness, Role-Related Knowledge.

Key Google questions:
- "Tell me about a time you had to learn something completely new in a short time."
- "Describe a time you made a decision that your team disagreed with. What happened?"
- "Tell me about a project where you had to work cross-functionally."
- "Give an example of a time you had to be creative to solve a problem."

## Story Templates to Build

### The "Fired the Customer" Story
Situation where you made the right technical/product decision despite pushback.
> "We had a customer requesting a feature that would have required us to fundamentally compromise the architecture. I pushed back, proposed an alternative that met 80% of their need, and ultimately we kept the customer but on our terms. This reinforced [LP]."

### The "On-Call at 3AM" Story
Demonstrates ownership, bias for action, and follow-through.
> "An alert fired at 3AM. I was oncall and within X minutes had the service restored. Root cause was Y. I wrote the post-mortem, fixed the underlying issue, and added Z monitoring to catch it earlier next time."

### The "I Was Wrong" Story
Demonstrates humility, earn trust, learn and be curious.
> "I was confident X approach was correct and pushed hard for it. Two weeks in, data showed Y was happening that my model hadn't predicted. I went back to the team, acknowledged the mistake, and we pivoted to Z. We recovered [quantified]."

### The "Drove Alignment" Story
Demonstrates leadership without authority, think big, disagree and commit.
> "Three teams had conflicting approaches to the same problem. I organized a working group, created a comparison doc, facilitated 3 discussions, and we landed on a unified approach that all three teams adopted. This saved [X weeks/Y cost] in duplicated work."

## Preparation Checklist

**Stories to prepare (minimum 8-10 total):**
- [ ] 2 technical challenges you're proud of (with numbers)
- [ ] 2 conflicts/disagreements (one where you won, one where you didn't)
- [ ] 2 failures/mistakes (real ones, not humble-brags)
- [ ] 1 time you led without authority
- [ ] 1 time you made a tough prioritization call
- [ ] 1 time you identified and fixed a problem proactively

**For each story, know:**
- [ ] The numbers (% improvement, time saved, users affected, scale)
- [ ] Your specific contribution (not the team's)
- [ ] What you would do differently
- [ ] Which LPs it maps to

**Day before:**
- [ ] Review your stories out loud — timing should be 2-3 min each
- [ ] Research the company's current priorities / recent news
- [ ] Prepare 3-5 thoughtful questions to ask at the end

## Questions to Ask the Interviewer

These signal curiosity, strategic thinking, and genuine interest:

**About engineering culture:**
- "What does good engineering look like at your team, vs. just shipping fast?"
- "How do teams handle technical debt — is there dedicated time or is it worked in?"
- "What's the biggest technical challenge the team is facing right now?"

**About the role:**
- "What would success look like in the first 6 months?"
- "What's a project a new engineer could own in their first quarter?"
- "What's the most important thing for someone in this role to get right?"

**About growth:**
- "How have people on this team grown from this role?"
- "What kind of mentorship or feedback culture exists here?"

**Never ask:**
- Anything easily Googled
- Salary/benefits in a coding interview (save for recruiter)
- "What does your company do?" (do your homework)
